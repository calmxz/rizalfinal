Early Life

During Rizal's era, travel was a luxury accessible to only a few due to its expense and limited transportation options. Unlike today's swift air travel, people relied on steamers, horses, trains, and walking to traverse distances.

Rizal wasn't just a tourist; he was a scholar of the cultures he encountered, traveling to expand his knowledge, particularly in the fields of science and literature. This article will delve into Rizal's various journeys, the wisdom he gained, friendships forged, places visited, and events witnessed.

It will explore his fascination with different cultures alongside his critical observations. Moreover, it will highlight how Rizal's accumulation of knowledge and skills contributed to the eventual downfall of Spain's 333-year reign in the Philippines.

Rizal's Hometown - Calamba, Laguna

Childhood Travels

During Rizal's birth, his mother nearly died due to his large head, prompting his family to promise the Virgin of Antipolo that if she aided his mother's recovery, they would take him to her sanctuary. His journey began in Calamba, where he delighted in moonlit walks. Accompanied by his father, he embarked on his first pilgrimage to Antipolo in 1868, leaving behind his mother, who had just given birth to Trinidad.

Crossing Laguna de Bay on a casco for the first time, Rizal was awestruck by the vast water and the serenity of the night, staying awake in wonder. After visiting the Virgin of Antipolo and Saturnina in Manila, they returned to Calamba, where Rizal occasionally reflected on the shore, laying the groundwork for his adventurous spirit.

Laguna de Bay

Schooling in Biñan

In June 1869, on a Sunday afternoon, amidst tearful farewells from his family, he departed Calamba for Biñan, accompanied by his brother Paciano, in a carromata, a light, two-wheeled vehicle drawn by a native pony. After a brief drive, they reached their aunt's house, where Jose was to stay, arriving almost at nightfall. That same evening, his cousin Leandro explored the town.

His life in Biñan was marked by simplicity and routine, where he encountered his first school fight, took painting lessons, and excelled academically. Eventually, as his schooling concluded, he received news from his sister Saturnine about the arrival of the steamer Talim, which would transport him back to Calamba.

After bidding farewell to his teachers and classmates, he left Biñan on a Saturday afternoon in December 1870, having spent a year and a half there. During the journey aboard the steamer Talim, which filled him with excitement, he encountered a Frenchman, a friend of his father, who looked after him.

Biñan, Laguna

Schooling at Ateneo de Manila

Following the martyrdom of Gom-Bur-Za, Jose ventured to Manila to pursue his studies, enrolling at Ateneo Municipal. His inaugural day commenced with a Mass at the college chapel.

Despite starting at the bottom of his class, by month's end, he ascended to the rank of 'emperor'. After the academic year concluded in March 1873, Rizal returned to Calamba for summer break, where he visited his imprisoned mother and was warmly embraced by her.

Upon resuming studies in Manila for his second year, he prophesied his mother's liberation, a prediction that materialized. Engrossed by Dr. Feodor Jagor's "Travels in the Philippines", Rizal was captivated by Jagor's critique of Spanish colonization and his foresight of Spain's eventual loss of the Philippines.

In his third year, news of his mother's release buoyed his spirits, and by his fourth year, he resided as an interno at Ateneo. Encouraged by his professor Fr. Francisco de Paula Sanchez, Rizal delved deeper into his studies and poetry writing. Graduating in 1877 with distinction in all subjects, he concluded his schooling journey.

Ateneo During the Spanish Regime

Medical Studies at
University of Santo Tomas

After completing his first year in Philosophy and Letters, Rizal switched to the medical course. Despite his mother's objections, his father and brother Paciano supported his decision. At the University of Santo Tomas, he continued his education, winning literary accolades and engaging in romantic pursuits.

During his time there, he encountered the brutality of a Spanish officer, fell in love with Miss L, and penned 'To the Filipino Youth'.

In May 1881, Rizal embarked on a pilgrimage to Pakil, accompanied by his sisters and their friends. They traveled by Casco from Calamba to Pakil, Laguna, where they were captivated by the renowned turumba, witnessing the fervent street dances during the procession honoring the miraculous Birhen Maria de los Dolores.

University of Santo Tomas
During the Spanish Regime

Course to Spain

Continuing his studies at the University of Santo Tomas, Rizal grew disillusioned with the Dominican institution's teaching methods and the racial bias of its professors towards Filipino students. Upon completing the fourth year of his medical course, he decided to pursue further studies abroad, despite predicting his parents' disapproval and choosing not to seek their blessing.

Besides his academic pursuits in Spain, Rizal undertook a clandestine mission to meticulously observe European life, culture, languages, customs, industries, commerce, government, and laws. This mission aimed to equip him for the monumental task of liberating his oppressed compatriots from Spanish tyranny. The journey to Spain marked the beginning of Rizal's extensive travels.

Departure for Spain

To evade detection by Spanish authorities and friars, Rizal's departure for Spain was shrouded in secrecy, even from his own parents who would have objected. Only a select few, including his older brother, uncle, sisters Neneng and Lucia, the Valenzuela family, Pedro Paterno, Mateo Evangelista, the Ateneo Jesuit fathers, and close friends, were privy to his plans.

The Jesuit priests provided him with letters of recommendation to their counterparts in Barcelona, where he adopted the pseudonym Jose Mercado. Before leaving, he penned farewell letters to his beloved parents and sweetheart, Leonor Rivera.

On May 3, 1882, Rizal embarked on the Spanish steamer Salvadora bound for Singapore. With tears in his eyes and a heavy heart, he watched Manila's skyline fade into the distance, capturing the moment in a sketch as it disappeared from view.

Barcelona, Spain

Singapore

On May 3, 1882, during the voyage, Rizal attentively observed his fellow passengers and the ship's surroundings aboard the steamer.

Among the sixteen passengers, he was the lone Filipino, accompanied by Spaniards, British individuals, and Indian Negroes. Building a friendship with the ship's captain, Donato Lecha, Rizal engaged in chess matches to alleviate boredom, consistently proving his prowess in the game.

Upon the Salvadora's arrival in Singapore on May 9, Rizal lodged at Hotel de la Paz and embarked on a two-day exploration of the city.

His sightseeing excursion included visits to the renowned Botanical Garden, captivating Buddhist temples, bustling shopping districts, and the statue of Sir Thomas Stamford Raffles, the city's founder.

Salvadora

To Colombo

After a brief stay in Singapore, Rizal embarked on the French steamer Djemnah bound for Europe on May 11. Compared to his previous vessel, the Djemnah was larger and cleaner, accommodating more passengers.

Despite encountering challenges in communicating with the predominantly French-speaking passengers, Rizal attempted to converse in French, only to realize that his proficiency was insufficient. Resorting to a mixture of Spanish, Latin, and sketching on paper, he managed to communicate effectively and gradually improved his grasp of the French language through daily interactions with fellow passengers.

On May 17, the Djemnah reached Point Galle, a coastal town in southern Ceylon, which failed to impress Rizal. Continuing their voyage, they arrived at Colombo, the capital of Ceylon, the next day. Rizal was captivated by Colombo's scenic beauty and elegant architecture, finding the city to be truly remarkable.

Djemnah

Through Suez Canal

Continuing its journey from Colombo, the Djemnah sailed across the Indian Ocean towards the Cape coast of Africa, where Rizal caught sight of the barren shoreline for the first time, describing it as an 'inhospitable land but famous'.

Their next port of call was Aden, where Rizal experienced sweltering heat surpassing that of Manila and marveled at his first encounter with camels. Subsequently, the ship made its way to Suez, a pivotal city at the Red Sea terminal of the Suez Canal.

Upon disembarking, Rizal explored the city, particularly enchanted by the serene moonlight reminiscent of his hometown Calamba and family. It took five days for the Djemnah to traverse the Suez Canal, a thrilling experience for Rizal as he journeyed through a canal built by Ferdinand de Lesseps.

Upon reaching Port Said, Rizal disembarked once again to explore the city's captivating sights and was intrigued by the diverse inhabitants conversing in a multitude of languages.

Suez Canal

Naples and Marseilles

Departing from Port Said, the ship continued its voyage towards Europe. On June 11, Rizal arrived in Naples, a city that charmed him with its bustling business activity, vibrant populace, and picturesque scenery.

He was particularly captivated by the sight of Mount Vesuvius, the Castle of St. Elmo, and other historic landmarks. The following night, on June 12, the steamer docked at the French harbor of Marseilles.

Rizal bid farewell to his fellow passengers and embarked on a visit to the renowned Chateau d'If, famously associated with the imprisonment of Dantes. He spent two and a half days exploring the sights of Marseilles before continuing his journey.

Mount Vesuvius

Castle of St. Elmo

Barcelona

On the afternoon of May 15, Rizal departed from Marseilles by train, bound for Spain. Crossing the Pyrenees, he made a stopover in the frontier town of Port Bou for passport inspection before continuing his journey by rail.

Finally arriving in Barcelona on June 16, 1882, Rizal's initial impression of the city was unfavorable, deeming it ugly, dirty, and its residents inhospitable. However, his perception gradually changed, and he grew to appreciate Barcelona as a vibrant city exuding an atmosphere of freedom and liberalism. He found its people to be open-hearted, hospitable, and courageous, and enjoyed leisurely strolls along Las Ramblas, the famous street in Barcelona.

Welcomed by fellow Filipinos, who were former classmates from Ateneo, Rizal was treated to a party at Café Plaza de Cataluña, where he shared the latest news and gossip from the Philippines.

During his time in Barcelona, Rizal penned a nationalistic essay titled "Amor Patrio," his first written work on Spanish soil, which he sent to Basilio Teodoro Moran, publisher of Diariong Tagalog. Impressed by the article, Moran encouraged Rizal to write more.

Amidst living in Barcelona, Rizal received distressing news of a cholera outbreak devastating Manila and the provinces, causing many deaths and leaving his beloved Leonor Rivera visibly affected by his absence.

Additionally, prompted by advice from his brother Paciano, Rizal decided to continue his medical studies in Madrid, leading him to depart from Barcelona in the fall of 1882.

Las Ramblas in Barcelona, Spain

Diariong Tagalog

Madrid

On November 3, 1882, Rizal commenced his studies at the Universidad Central de Madrid, pursuing courses in Medicine and Philosophy and Letters.

In addition to his academic endeavors, he dedicated himself to studying painting and sculpture at the Academy of Fine Arts of San Fernando, while also undertaking private lessons in French, German, and English. Furthermore, he diligently practiced fencing and shooting at the Hall of Arms of Sanz y Carbonell.

Adopting a modest lifestyle in Madrid, Rizal remained focused on his purpose of preparing himself for service to his homeland. He managed his finances and time meticulously, abstaining from gambling, indulgence in wine, and frivolous pursuits with women.

On Saturday evenings, he frequented the home of Don Pablo Ortiga y Rey, a former mayor of Manila, where he formed a close relationship with Don Pablo, his son, and daughter. Rizal eventually became romantically involved with Consuelo Ortiga y Perez, Don Pablo's daughter, drawn to her beauty and vivacity amid his loneliness in a foreign land.

However, their love affair faced challenges as Rizal was still engaged to Leonor Rivera, and a close friend of his was also enamored with Consuelo.

Universidad Central de Madrid

First Visit to Paris

In June 1883, Rizal departed Madrid for Paris, where he initially stayed at the Hotel de Paris before relocating to more affordable accommodations. Like any tourist, he marveled at the enchanting sights of Paris, including the picturesque boulevards, the Opera House, Place de la Concorde, the Arc de Triomphe, Bois de Boulogne, the Madeleine Church, Notre Dame Cathedral, the Vendome Column, Les Invalides, and Versailles. He immersed himself in French culture, spending countless hours exploring museums and closely observing the French way of life.

During his time in Spain, Rizal forged close ties with influential Spanish liberals and republicans, many of whom were Freemasons. Impressed by their outspoken criticism of government policies and the friars, Rizal joined the Masonic lodge Acacia in Madrid in March 1883, seeking their support in his struggle against the friars in the Philippines. He later transferred to Lodge Solidaridad, where he attained the rank of Master Mason on November 15, 1890, and received a diploma from Le Grand Orient de France in Paris.

Meanwhile, back in Calamba, circumstances worsened as droughts and locusts devastated harvests, exacerbating the financial strain on Rizal's family due to increased rents imposed by the Dominican-owned hacienda. Consequently, Rizal often experienced delays or even lack of allowances, causing significant hardship.

In November 1884, Rizal participated in student demonstrations advocating for academic freedom, particularly in support of Dr. Miguel Morayta's liberal views, which were denounced by the Catholic bishops of Spain.

On June 21, 1884, Rizal completed his medical studies in Spain, receiving the degree of Licentiate in Medicine from the Universidad Central de Madrid. In the following academic year, he successfully passed all subjects leading to the degree of Doctor of Medicine, while also achieving excellent ratings in his studies in Philosophy and Letters.

Paris to Berlin

After completing his studies in Spain, Rizal pursued specialization in ophthalmology, motivated by his desire to alleviate his mother's worsening eye condition. Despite this focus on medicine, he remained committed to his "secret mission" of observing European customs and lifestyles to serve his homeland in the future.

In 1885, at the age of 24, Rizal left the Central University of Madrid and traveled to Paris to further his knowledge in ophthalmology. Along the way, he stopped in Barcelona to visit his friend Maximo Viola, also a medical student from a wealthy family in Bulacan.

Arriving in Paris in November of that year, Rizal spent approximately four months there, apprenticing under Dr. Louis de Wecker, a renowned French ophthalmologist, which significantly enhanced his expertise in the field. When not at Dr. Wecker's clinic, Rizal socialized with friends, including the Pardo de Taveras family, Juan Luna, and Felix Resurreccion Hidalgo.

He particularly enjoyed spending time in Luna's studio, where they discussed art and Rizal even posed for some of Luna's paintings, notably appearing as one of the Egyptian priests in Luna's work "The Death of Cleopatra."

Dr. Louis de Wecker

At Heidelberg

Leaving Paris on February 1, 1886, after gaining valuable experience at Dr. Wecker's clinic, Rizal embarked on a journey to Germany. Making stops in Strasbourg and other German towns, he arrived in Heidelberg on February 3, 1886. This historic city, renowned for its ancient university and picturesque surroundings, became his temporary home.

Initially lodging with German law students in a boarding house, Rizal's adeptness at chess earned him membership in the Chess Player's Club. He later moved to a boarding house near the University of Heidelberg, where he worked at the University Eye Hospital under the guidance of Dr. Otto Becker and attended lectures by Dr. Becker and Prof. Wilhelm Kuehne.

During weekends, he explored the scenic spots around Heidelberg, including the majestic Heidelberg Castle, the enchanting Neckar River, theaters, and old churches. Rizal admired the harmonious coexistence of German Catholics and Protestants, noting their practice of ecumenism.

On April 22, 1886, amid the springtime bloom in Heidelberg, Rizal penned a poem to the vibrant flowers along the Neckar River, particularly cherishing the forget-me-nots. He then enjoyed a three-month summer vacation in Wilhelmsfeld, a quaint mountain village near Heidelberg, residing at the vicarage of the kind Protestant pastor, Dr. Karl Ullmer.

On July 31, 1886, Rizal composed his first letter in German to Professor Ferdinand Blumentritt, intrigued by Blumentritt's interest in Philippine languages. Along with the letter, Rizal sent a copy of Aritmetica. Impressed by Rizal's gesture, Blumentritt reciprocated by sending him gift books, marking the beginning of their enduring correspondence.

Rizal was fortunate to witness the fifth centenary celebration of the University of Heidelberg on August 6, 1886, just days before his departure, a bittersweet occasion as he had grown fond of the land and the beautiful city.

Heidelberg

Leipzig and Dresden

On August 9, 1886, three days following the fifth centenary celebration of the University of Heidelberg, Rizal departed the city, boarding a train to embark on a journey across various cities in Germany.

Arriving in Leipzig on August 14, 1886, he attended lectures at the University of Leipzig and forged friendships with Professor Friedrich Ratzel, a renowned German historian, and Dr. Hans Meyer, a German anthropologist. During his time in Leipzig, Rizal undertook the task of translating William Tell from German to Filipino, aiming to familiarize Filipinos with the story of the Swiss champion of independence.

Additionally, he translated Hans Christian Andersen's Fairy Tales into Filipino. Benefiting from the city's affordable cost of living, Rizal remained in Leipzig for two and a half months, utilizing his time to revise chapters of his second novel and engage in physical exercise.

He also worked as a proof-reader at a publishing firm, earning additional income. Departing Leipzig on October 29, 1886, Rizal proceeded to Dresden, where he briefly met Dr. Adolph Meyer, the director of the Anthropological and Ethnological Museum, before continuing his journey.

Despite his brief stay in Dresden, Rizal was deeply moved by the music during a Catholic Mass he attended, describing it as profoundly sublime. On the morning of November 1, 1886, Rizal departed Dresden by train, arriving in Berlin later that evening.

University of Leipzig

Dresden

Dresden

Berlin

Rizal found Berlin appealing for its scientific atmosphere and lack of racial prejudice. Here, he had the opportunity to meet Dr. Feodor Jagor, author of Travels in the Philippines, a work Rizal greatly admired for its insightful observations on the Philippines.

Through Dr. Jagor, Rizal was introduced to Dr. Rudolf Virchow, a renowned anthropologist, and his son, Dr. Hans Virchow, a professor of Descriptive Anatomy. Rizal also worked alongside Dr. Karl Ernest Schweigger, a prominent German ophthalmologist, during his time in Berlin.

His accomplishments in Berlin were remarkable, becoming the first Asian honored with membership in prestigious societies such as the Anthropological Society, the Ethnological Society, and the Geographical Society of Berlin.

Recognizing Rizal's intellectual prowess, Dr. Virchow invited him to deliver a lecture before the Ethnographic Society of Berlin, where Rizal presented a scholarly paper titled Taglische Verkunst (Tagalog Metrical Art), receiving acclaim from the scientific community.

Despite his rigorous academic pursuits, Rizal led a disciplined life in Berlin, balancing his assistant duties during the day with attending lectures at night. He maintained his physical fitness through daily exercise and diligently practiced languages such as German, French, and Italian.

Rizal also took private French lessons under Madame Lucie Cerdole to master the language. In his leisure moments, Rizal explored the countryside surrounding Berlin, immersing himself in the local culture and sketching his observations. He was particularly captivated by German Yuletide customs, such as decorating pine tree branches with lanterns, papers, and candies.

Additionally, Rizal found the German tradition of self-introduction intriguing, where individuals would bow their heads and personally greet each guest in a room. However, not all experiences in Germany were favorable for Rizal.

During one harsh winter, he endured poverty when financial support from Calamba ceased, leaving him with only one meal a day and forcing him to wash his own clothes due to lack of funds for laundry. Despite efforts by his brother Paciano to raise funds, the failure of crops and collapse of the sugar market in Calamba exacerbated their financial struggles.

Noli Me Tangere published in Berlin

During his time in Berlin, Rizal faced challenges in publishing "Noli Me Tangere," but with the assistance of Maximo Viola, who provided both funds for publication and living expenses, the novel became a reality. As they joyfully celebrated Christmas in 1886, the chief of police's sudden visit to Rizal's boarding house raised concerns.

Unable to produce a passport, Rizal faced the threat of deportation. Despite his efforts, Rizal failed to obtain a passport within the given timeframe, but his explanation of being a Filipino physician and scientist, not a French spy, convinced the authorities to allow him to remain in Germany.

When "Noli Me Tangere" was finally printed on March 21, 1887, Rizal promptly shared copies with close friends, dedicating the novel to the Philippines. Its profound depiction of the country's struggles resonated deeply with readers, including influential figures like Blumentritt, who praised the novel's authenticity and heartfelt narrative, recognizing it as a significant literary achievement.

Grand Tour of Europe

Following the publication of "Noli Me Tangere," Rizal embarked on a journey across Europe, intending to visit significant locales. With financial support from Paciano amounting to 1,000 pesos, Rizal promptly repaid Viola 300 pesos of his generous loan.

On the morning of May 11, 1887, Rizal and Viola departed Berlin by train, ushered by the arrival of spring and the blossoming landscapes of Europe. Their first destination was Dresden, renowned as one of the finest cities in Germany.

Rizal's Hometown - Calamba, Laguna

Dresden

During their stay in Dresden, Rizal and Viola immersed themselves in the regional floral exposition, coinciding with their visit. Rizal, with a keen interest in botany, devoted time to studying various plant species. Their itinerary included a visit to Dr. Adolph B. Meyer, whose delight at their presence was evident.

Exploring the Museum of Art, Rizal was particularly struck by the painting "Prometheus Bound," depicting the Greek mythological tragedy.

A chance encounter with Dr. Jagor while strolling through the floral exposition prompted advice to inform Blumentritt of their impending visit, considering the professor's nervous disposition. Following this advice, Rizal and Viola sent a wire to Blumentritt from their next stopover in Teschen.

Rizal's Hometown - Calamba, Laguna

Blumentritt and Leitmeritz

On May 13, 1887, at 1:30 p.m., Rizal and Viola arrived at Leitmeritz railroad station, where they were warmly welcomed by Professor Blumentritt, who recognized them thanks to a sketch Rizal had previously sent.

Their meeting marked the beginning of a profound friendship, with Blumentritt immediately taking Rizal to heart. During their stay, they enjoyed the hospitality of Blumentritt's family, relishing the cooking of his wife Rosa and cherishing moments spent with their children.

Blumentritt introduced them to the scenic and historical wonders of Leitmeritz, including a visit to a local beer garden where Rizal's fluency in German left the townsfolk astonished. They also attended a meeting of the Tourists’ Club, where Rizal's linguistic skills garnered further admiration.

As a gesture of gratitude, Rizal painted a portrait of Blumentritt, and they bid farewell to their gracious host and his family on May 16, carrying with them cherished memories of their time in Leitmeritz.

History City of Prague

After departing from Leitmeritz, Rizal and Viola journeyed to Prague, bearing recommendation letters from Blumentritt to Professor Willkomm of the University of Prague.

Welcomed warmly by the professor and his family, they explored the city's historical landmarks, including the tomb of Copernicus and the museum of natural history.

They also visited notable sites such as the bacteriological laboratories and the cave associated with San Juan Nepomuceno. Following their stay with the Willkomms, Rizal and Viola continued their travels, moving on to Brunn.

Queen of the Danube

Upon their arrival in Vienna on May 20, Rizal and Viola were captivated by the city's renowned beauty, immortalized in song and tale. Entrusted with a letter of recommendation from Blumentritt, they met with Norfenfals, a celebrated novelist of the time, who was deeply impressed by Rizal's intellect and talents.

Meanwhile, in a stroke of good fortune, Rizal's lost diamond stickpin was recovered by a kind soul at Hotel Krebs and returned to him via Blumentritt. During their stay at Hotel Metropole, they immersed themselves in the rich cultural tapestry of Vienna, exploring its churches, museums, art galleries, theaters, and parks.

Lintz

On May 24, Rizal and Viola embarked from Vienna on a river boat expedition along the picturesque Danube Rivera. En route, Rizal keenly observed the diverse sights unfolding before them: barges laden with goods, vibrant flowers and foliage lining the riverbanks, boats serving as homes for families, and idyllic villages dotting the riversides. They also noted the passengers' use of paper napkins during meals, adding a touch of modern convenience to their journey.

To Rheinfall, To Salzburg,
To Munich & Nuremberg

The river journey concluded in Lintz, leading them to travel overland to Salzburg and then onward to Munich, where they indulged in the renowned Munich beer, celebrated as the finest in Germany.

Continuing their travels, they reached Nuremberg, an ancient city rich in history, where they were struck by the chilling remnants of the Inquisition's torture machines and fascinated by the craftsmanship of doll making.

Their itinerary then took them to Ulm, where they marveled at the grandeur of the city's cathedral, climbing its countless steps with Viola feeling dizzy while Rizal remained unfazed.

Their journey continued through Stuttgart, Baden, and finally to Rheinfall, where they beheld the breathtaking spectacle of Europe's most magnificent waterfall.

Rizal's Hometown - Calamba, Laguna

Switzerland

After crossing into Schaffhausen, Switzerland from Rheinfall, Rizal and Viola spent a brief stay from June 2 to 3, 1887, before journeying onward to Basel, Bern, and Lausanne.

Following their exploration of Lausanne, they embarked on a boat across the misty Leman Lake to Geneva. Geneva, renowned for its beauty and frequented by tourists worldwide, captivated Rizal and Viola.

The city's inhabitants were polyglots, fluent in French, German, and Italian, with Rizal engaging in conversation in all three languages. They enjoyed boating on the lake, where Rizal showcased his skill in rowing acquired during his youth in Calamba.

On June 19, 1887, Rizal celebrated his 26th birthday by treating Viola to a grand celebration. Spending a total of fifteen days in Geneva, Rizal and Viola bid farewell on June 23, with Viola opting to return to Barcelona while Rizal continued his journey to Italy.

Rizal's Hometown - Calamba, Laguna

Madrid Exposition

The sad news from his friends in Madrid regarding the deplorable conditions of the primitive Igorots being exhibited at expositions deeply affected Rizal. Hearing that some of them had died, and witnessing the inappropriate treatment they endured, including mockery of their clothing and weapons, stirred Rizal's outrage. As a champion of human dignity, he could not stand by while such disrespect was shown to these indigenous people.

Rizal's Hometown - Calamba, Laguna

Italy

Rizal's journey through Italy took him to Turin, Milan, Venice, and Florence before arriving in Rome on June 27, 1887. The grandeur and historical significance of the Eternal City left him thrilled and captivated. On June 29th, he had his first visit to the Vatican, the center of Catholicism and the seat of the Pope.

The magnificence of St. Peter's Church and the Vatican's other structures made a profound impression on him, especially as it coincided with the feast day of St. Peter.

Despite his exhaustion from days filled with sightseeing, Rizal found solace in his rest at the hotel. In a letter to Blumentritt, he humorously expressed his fatigue, likening it to that of a dog's, yet anticipated the rejuvenation of sleep.

With his return to the Philippines looming, Rizal informed his father of his impending homecoming after a week spent absorbing the wonders of Rome.

Rizal's Hometown - Calamba, Laguna

First Homecoming

From 1882 to 1887, Rizal immersed himself in European studies, cherishing the enchanting memories of his journey. Yet, his love for his homeland and nationality remained steadfast.

Despite the dangers posed by the uproar caused by his novel "Noli Me Tangere," particularly among the friars, Rizal resolved to return to the Philippines in August 1887. His motivations included the desire to restore his mother's sight and to gauge the impact of his literary work on Filipino society.

Departing from Rome by train to Marseilles, he embarked once again on the steamer Djemnah, retracing his route from five years prior, traversing the Suez Canal for the second time.

Subsequently, on July 30, he transferred to the steamer Haiphong in Saigon, bound for Manila, departing on August 2.

Rizal's Hometown - Calamba, Laguna

Arrival at Manila

Arriving in Manila on August 5 aboard the Haiphong, Rizal disembarked with joy, grateful to be back on Filipino soil. He spent a brief period in the city, visiting friends and noting that Manila appeared unchanged from his departure five years earlier.

Rizal's Hometown - Calamba, Laguna

Home in Calamba

Returning to Calamba on August 8, Rizal was warmly welcomed by his family, though their joy was tempered by concerns for his safety.

Paciano, ever vigilant, remained by his side to shield him from potential threats, while even Rizal's father insisted on accompanying him whenever he ventured out. Setting up a medical clinic in Calamba, his first patient was his nearly blind mother, drawing attention as news spread of the skilled doctor returned from Germany.

Patients from Manila and beyond sought his consultations, earning him a substantial income within a month. Additionally, he established a gymnasium to promote European sports and discourage local vices like cockfighting and gambling.

Despite his efforts, Rizal was unable to reunite with Leonor Rivera, his beloved. Shortly after his return, he received a letter from Governor General Emilio Terrero, summoning him to Malacañan Palace.

At the meeting, Rizal defended himself against accusations stemming from his novel, "Noli Me Tangere," asserting that it merely portrayed truths and not seditious ideas.

Pleased with his explanation, Terrero requested a copy of the book, which Rizal promised to provide. Recognizing the danger Rizal faced from powerful friars, Terrero assigned a Spanish lieutenant as his bodyguard for protection.

Farewell Again

Rizal's novel provoked outrage among the friars, leading to anonymous threats against his life that deeply concerned his parents. Recognizing the danger, they urged him to leave for his own safety. Governor General Terrero intervened, advising Rizal to depart the Philippines to avoid the fury of the friars' vengeance. Although reluctant, Rizal felt compelled to comply with the governor general's directive. Thus, in 1888, he left Calamba behind.

Hong Kong

Haunted by enemies and threatened by the influence of the friars, Rizal was compelled to depart the Philippines once again in February 1888, at the age of 27. He carried with him the weight of disillusionment and frustration, a victim of the injustices of humanity and a thwarted advocate for reform.

Setting sail aboard the Zafiro on February 3, 1888, after a six-month stay in Calamba, Rizal endured illness and melancholy during the turbulent voyage across the China Sea, opting to remain on board during a stopover at Amoy due to sickness and unfavorable weather conditions.

Arriving in Hong Kong on February 8, he took up residence at the Victoria Hotel, where he received a warm welcome from the Filipino community.

However, his movements in Hong Kong were closely monitored by a Spaniard named Jose Varanda, believed to be spying on Rizal under orders.

Macao

On February 18, accompanied by Basa, Rizal embarked on the ferry steamer Kiu-Kiang bound for Macao, where he was surprised to encounter the familiar figure of Sainz de Varanda among the passengers.

Describing Macao as small, low, and somber, Rizal noted the prevalence of junks and sampans but few steamers, giving the impression of a desolate atmosphere. They lodged at the home of Don Juan Francisco Lecaros, married to a Portuguese woman, during their two-day stay.

Exploring the city, Rizal visited various attractions including the theater, casino, cathedral, churches, pagodas, botanical gardens, and bazaars, and also observed the famous Grotto of Camoens.

On the evening of February 19, he witnessed a Catholic procession, where devotees clad in blue and purple attire carried unlit candles.

Returning to Hong Kong on February 20 aboard the ferry steamer Kiu-Kiang, Rizal's journey continued amidst his exploration of new lands and experiences.

Return to Hong Kong

During his two-week stay in Hong Kong, Rizal immersed himself in studying the Chinese way of life, language, drama, and customs.

He meticulously documented his observations in his diary, noting the vibrant celebrations of the Chinese New Year from February 11th to 13th, characterized by incessant firecracker explosions which he enthusiastically participated in.

He also experienced the lively Chinese theater, attended the marathon Lauriat party, known as the world's longest meal, and took note of the wealth of the Dominican Order and the local cemeteries.

On February 22, 1888, Rizal departed Hong Kong aboard the Oceanic, an American steamer bound for Japan. Although unimpressed by the meals onboard, he appreciated the ship's cleanliness and efficient management during the voyage.

Japan

One of the happiest periods in Rizal's life was his time in Japan, where he spent one and a half months from February 28 to April 13, 1888.

Captivated by Japan's natural beauty, the manners of its people, and the picturesque shrines, he found solace and romance amidst his sorrows, even falling in love with a Japanese woman whose loveliness brought joy to his heart.

Arriving in Yokohama on the morning of February 28, he lodged at the Grand Hotel before moving to Tokyo the next day, where he stayed at the Tokyo Hotel until March 7.

Despite his initial struggles with the language, he soon began studying Japanese, eventually becoming conversant within a few days.

Invited by Juan Perez Caballero, secretary of the Spanish Legation, to stay at the Spanish Legation, Rizal accepted, aware of the Spanish government's monitoring but forming a close friendship with Caballero nonetheless, whom he described as young, refined, and a talented writer.

Immersing himself in Japanese culture, Rizal studied drama, arts, music, and judo, exploring museums, libraries, art galleries, and shrines, and visiting picturesque villages like Meguro, Nikko, Hakone, and Miyanoshita.

Impressed by a performance of Western music by the Tokyo band in a park, he was surprised to discover they were Filipino musicians and conversed with them in Tagalog.

Despite his admiration for Japan's natural beauty, the cleanliness, politeness of its people, and the charm of its women, he disliked the use of rickshaws drawn by men, finding it distasteful.

Sayonara Japan and Seiko Usui

During his time in Japan, Rizal encountered a charming Japanese woman named Seiko Usui, whom he affectionately called O-Sei-San. Their shared interests in the arts ignited a deep connection and blossoming romance.

Entranced by Seiko's beauty, charm, modesty, and intelligence, Rizal found happiness in her company and was tempted to settle down in Japan, especially given the enticing job offer from the Spanish Legation.

However, his unwavering love for his homeland and his mission to liberate his oppressed people compelled him to reconsider. Though their love was profound, Rizal ultimately made the difficult decision to depart from Japan.

Boarding the Belgic, an English steamer, on April 13, 1888, from Yokohama bound for the United States, Rizal left Japan with a heavy heart, knowing he would never again behold its beauty or his beloved O-Sei-San.

Despite the sorrow of parting, his 45-day sojourn in Japan remained one of the happiest chapters of his life, cherished in his memories forever.

Across the Pacific

Despite his sorrowful heart, Rizal found solace in the pleasant trans-Pacific voyage to the United States. During the journey, a child on board approached him, asking if he knew a man named "Richal" in Manila, to which Rizal amusingly replied that he was "Richal" himself.

The child's excitement at discovering they were traveling with a celebrity brought pride to their mother. Additionally, Rizal formed a bond with Tetcho Suehiro, a Japanese journalist and advocate for human rights, who was forced to leave Japan.

Initially alone due to his proficiency in Japanese, Rizal befriended Tetcho and acted as his interpreter throughout their voyage from Yokohama to San Francisco, across the U.S. to New York, and finally to London, where they parted ways.

Sharing his life story and mission for emancipation from Spanish tyranny, Rizal inspired Tetcho, who in turn encouraged Rizal to strengthen his crusade for human rights in his homeland.

On December 1, 1888, after eight months of friendship, Rizal and Tetcho bid each other farewell with a final handshake, never to meet again.

Visit to the United States

Rizal's first encounter with America occurred on April 28, 1888, but his arrival was overshadowed by the stark reality of racial prejudice, witnessing discriminatory treatment towards Chinese and African American individuals by white Americans.

Despite this, he formed both positive and negative impressions of the United States. He admired the country's material progress, the vigor and determination of its people, the natural beauty of the landscape, the high standard of living, and the opportunities it offered to impoverished immigrants for a better life.

However, he was dismayed by the evident lack of racial equality, finding it contradictory to the democratic and freedom-loving principles espoused by Americans, which they often failed to uphold in practice. Rizal's brief trip to America lasted from April 28 to May 16, 1888.

San Francisco

Upon the steamer Belgic's arrival in San Francisco on Saturday morning, April 28, 1888, all passengers were barred from disembarking due to quarantine measures imposed by American health authorities, citing a supposed cholera epidemic originating from the Far East, a claim Rizal found dubious.

Alongside fellow passengers, he protested this unjustifiable action, later discovering it was politically motivated due to the presence of 653 Chinese coolies on board.

Finally permitted to go ashore on Friday afternoon, May 4, Rizal registered at the Palace Hotel, where he stayed for two days until May 6. Departing for Oakland on May 6 via ferry boat, he enjoyed breakfast in Reno, Nevada, the following morning.

Continuing his journey, he passed through various states, including Utah, Colorado, Nebraska, and Illinois, arriving in Chicago on May 11, where he noted the prevalence of cigar-selling stores adorned with Indian figures.

On May 12, he marveled at the beauty of Wagner Car and the Niagara Falls before reaching Albany on May 13, admiring the scenic Hudson River.

The transcontinental journey concluded on Sunday, May 13, when Rizal arrived in New York, where he spent three days exploring scenic and historic sites, including the awe-inspiring memorial of George Washington.

Departing for Liverpool on May 16, 1888, aboard the City of Rome, he bid farewell to New York, catching a glimpse of the colossal Statue of Liberty as the ship sailed away from the harbor, ending his memorable trip to America.

Once Again in London

Rizal settled in London from May 1888 to March 1889, motivated by three primary reasons.

Firstly, he sought to enhance his proficiency in the English language, recognizing its increasing importance in global communication and diplomacy.

Secondly, he aimed to study and annotate Antonio de Morga's "Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas," a historical work rumored to be accessible at the British Museum, furthering his research on Philippine history and colonialism.

Lastly, London provided a refuge from the oppressive reach of Spanish tyranny, offering Rizal a safe haven to pursue his intellectual endeavors without fear of persecution or reprisal.

Across the Atlantic

Rizal's trans-Atlantic voyage aboard the luxurious City of Rome proved to be an enjoyable experience, during which he garnered numerous friends from diverse nationalities owing to his adeptness as a linguist.

In addition to his linguistic talents, he entertained fellow passengers with his remarkable yo-yo skills, utilizing it not merely as a toy but also as an impressive offensive weapon.

Arriving in Liverpool, England in May 1888, Rizal spent one day in the city, lodging at the Adelphi Hotel. He described Liverpool as a sizable and aesthetically pleasing metropolis.

Life in London

On May 25, 1888, Rizal arrived in London, initially staying at the residence of Dr. Antonio Ma. Regidor, a lawyer. Shortly thereafter, he became a boarder with the Beckett family, whose home was conveniently located near public parks and within walking distance of the British Museum.

Immersing himself in research, Rizal dedicated most of his time at the museum, meticulously studying Morga's "Sucesos" and other rare historical texts pertaining to the Philippines.

His Sundays were often spent at the home of Dr. Rost, a Ministry of Foreign Affairs librarian renowned for his expertise in Malayan languages and customs, who was impressed by Rizal's knowledge and character, leading to recommendations to the British Museum authorities.

Dubbing Rizal "a pearl of a man," Dr. Rost spoke highly of him. Over the course of ten months in London, Rizal delved deeply into his historical studies, while his compatriots in Spain advocated for Philippine reforms.

In September 1888, he briefly visited Paris to scour the Bibliotheque Nationale for additional historical materials, enjoying the hospitality of Juan Luna and his wife during his stay.

After completing his research, he returned to London. Later, on December 11, 1888, Rizal journeyed to Spain, where he visited Madrid and Barcelona, connecting with fellow compatriots and assessing the political climate.

It was during this trip that he met Marcelo H. Del Pilar and Mariano Ponce, prominent figures in the Propaganda Movement, exchanging ideas and pledging cooperation in the struggle for reforms.

Christmas in London

Upon returning to London on December 24, Rizal celebrated Christmas and New Year's Day with the Beckett family, finding solace in the festive atmosphere and reflecting on cherished memories of his infancy intertwined with the significance of Christ's birth. Mrs. Beckett gifted him a book titled "The Life and Adventures of Valentine Vox, the Ventriloquist," adding to his literary collection.

During his London sojourn, Rizal assumed the role of honorary president of Asociacion La Solidaridad, a patriotic society advocating for reforms, which was inaugurated on December 31, 1888. He penned his inaugural article for La Solidaridad, titled "Los Agricultores Filipinos," published on March 25, 1889.

Amidst his busy schedule, Rizal authored several works, including "La Vision del Fray Rodriguez" and "Letter to the Young Women of Malolos," along with contributing articles to Dr. Rost's journal, "Specimens of Tagal Folklore" and "Two Eastern Fables."

Additionally, Rizal found himself enamored with Gertrude, one of the Beckett sisters. On March 19, 1889, bidding farewell to the Beckett family, Rizal departed London for Paris, filled with sadness as he left behind countless cherished memories in the city that had become a significant chapter in his life.

Universal Exposition of 1889

In the spring of 1889, Rizal journeyed to Paris, captivated by the city's bustling atmosphere spurred by the Universal Exposition. Accommodations were scarce due to the event's high demand, prompting landlords to hike rents.

Initially lodging with his friend Valentin Ventura, Rizal later found a place with two fellow Filipinos, Capitan Justo Trinidad and Jose Albert, after hopping between various hotels. Amidst the festivities, he became the godfather to the daughter of Juan Luna and Paz Pardo de Tavera, Maria de la Paz, born on June 24, 1889.

Entranced by the Expo's marvels, particularly the iconic Eiffel Tower, Rizal took part in an art competition but left empty-handed. He was also struck by the proud American Indians he encountered, urging his companions to emulate their unapologetic identity. Establishing the R.D.L.M. Society in Paris, Rizal aimed to disseminate knowledge and uplift the Malay race.

Delving deep into Philippine history, he penned "The Indolence of the Filipinos" and became a member of the "International Association of Filipinologists." Celebrating Christmas in Paris with Jose Albert, Rizal scraped together funds for a modest feast. After a brief New Year's visit to London, he returned to Paris, where the onset of influenza swept through the city.

In January 1890, he ventured to Brussels, accompanied by Jose Albert, to focus on completing his second novel, "El Filibusterismo." In Brussels, Rizal immersed himself in writing, contributing to La Solidaridad and engaging in medical activities and gymnastics.

Advocating for the Filipinization of the Tagalog orthography, Rizal remained committed to his homeland despite distressing news of agrarian strife and familial struggles.

Cherishing the summer festivities and a budding romance with Petite Jacoby, Rizal's time in Brussels was marked by both sorrow and moments of joy.

Ghent

In Ghent, Belgium, Rizal sought affordable printing options for his novel, "El Filibusterismo," owing to limited funds. After careful deliberation, he secured a deal with the F. Meyer-Van Loo Press, agreeing to pay in installments.

To meet the initial payment and subsequent partial payments, Rizal resorted to pawning all his jewels. However, when financial constraints threatened to halt the printing process, Ventura, upon learning of Rizal's plight, promptly provided the necessary funds.

With Ventura's assistance, the printing of "El Filibusterismo" resumed, marking a pivotal moment in Rizal's literary endeavors. In a gesture honoring the memory of the martyred priests Gom-Bur-Za, Rizal dedicated the novel to their noble sacrifice.

Back to Hong Kong

In November 1891, Rizal departed Europe for Hong Kong, where he resided until June 1892. Aboard the steamer Melbourne from Marseilles, he commenced writing his third novel in Tagalog during the voyage.

Among his unfinished works were "Makamisa," "Dapitan," and another untitled novel. Describing the journey as "heavenly," Rizal found himself the sole Asian among over 80 passengers, mostly Europeans, and two Spaniards bound for Amoy. His linguistic prowess impressed fellow passengers.

Arriving in Hong Kong on November 20, 1891, Rizal was welcomed by Jose Basa and took up residence at Number 5 D' Aguilar Street, where he also established his medical practice.

The Christmas of 1891 brought joy with the arrival of Rizal's father, brother, Silvestre Ubaldo, and later, his mother and sisters Lucia, Josefa, and Trinidad.

This family reunion marked one of the happiest moments in Rizal's life. In Hong Kong, Rizal practiced ophthalmology and gained renown as a skilled medical practitioner.

Second Homecoming

In May 1892, Rizal resolved to return to Manila, entrusting two sealed letters marked "to be opened after my death" to his friend Dr. Marques for safekeeping.

On June 26, 1892, Rizal, accompanied by his sister Lucia, arrived in Manila and lodged at the Hotel de Oriente facing Binondo Church. The following day, June 27, Rizal traveled by train from Tutuban Station to visit friends, and on July 6, he resumed his interviews at Malacañan Palace.

During one such visit, the governor general presented him with printed leaflets (Pobres Frailes) allegedly found in Lucia's pillowcases. Despite Rizal's denial and insistence on an investigation, he was arrested and escorted to Fort Santiago.

On July 15, 1892, Rizal was transferred to the steamer Cebu bound for Dapitan. Upon arrival on July 17, he was handed over to Captain Ricardo Carnicero, the commandant of Dapitan. Rizal's exile in Dapitan lasted until July 31, 1896, spanning four years.

Exile in Dapitan

Rizal spent four years in Dapitan, where he chose to live in Captain Carnicero's house rather than the Jesuit Church. The two developed a close friendship, with Carnicero impressed by Rizal's character.

On September 21, 1892, Rizal won 6,200 pesos in the Manila Lottery, which he shared with his father and friend Basa, using the remainder to invest in agricultural lands. During his time in Dapitan, Rizal engaged in debates about religion with Father Pastells and reconnected with his Ateneo teacher, Father Sanchez. Despite religious differences, they remained friends.

Rizal practiced medicine, offering free services to the poor and performing surgeries, including on his mother's eye. He also established a water system for Dapitan and taught local children while beautifying the town plaza.

An avid farmer, Rizal introduced modern agricultural methods and machinery to Dapitan. He also had a brief but happy relationship with Josephine Bracken, whom he married in Dapitan.

On July 31, 1896, Rizal's exile ended, and he boarded the steamer España with Josephine, family, and pupils, bidding farewell to the people of Dapitan. Rizal's last trip abroad began on August 1, with stops in Dumaguete, Cebu, Iloilo, and Romblon before sailing to Manila.

He then boarded the Spanish cruiser Castilla to Spain, where he was informed of his impending arrest by Governor General Blanco.

Arriving in Barcelona on October 3, Rizal was taken to Monjuich prison-fortress before being transferred to the ship Colon.

On October 6, he left Barcelona aboard the Colon under heavy guard.

Last Homecoming

Rizal's return to Manila on November 3 was met with anticipation from the Spanish authorities. He was transferred to Fort Santiago and underwent a preliminary investigation starting on November 20, facing 15 pieces of documentary evidence against him.

On November 26, the records were transmitted for further action. Given the right to choose his defense counsel, Rizal selected Don Luis Taviel de Andrade, whose brother had once served as his bodyguard in Calamba.

On December 13, General Camilo G. de Polavieja assumed the role of Governor General of the Philippines.

Feeling the weight of his circumstances, Rizal penned a manifesto on December 15, urging his people to seek liberation through education and industry rather than violence.

As Christmas arrived on December 25, 1896, Rizal found himself alone and despondent in his prison cell.

Trial and Death

On December 26, the trial of Rizal commenced, but it was far from fair. The court seemed predetermined to find him guilty, ignoring any evidence in his favor and hastily passing a sentence of death.

On December 28, General Polavieja ratified the court-martial's decision, scheduling Rizal's execution for December 30 at Bagumbayan Field. During his final days, Rizal was visited by loved ones, including Josephine, Jesuit priests, and friends.

He penned his last poem and a farewell letter to his best friend, hiding them in an alcohol cooking stove. On the morning of December 30, Rizal faced his execution with dignity.

Dressed in black, he kissed a crucifix and faced the firing squad, though his request to face them directly was denied.

Unfazed by fear, he met his fate with courage. As the drums sounded, the order to fire was given, and Rizal fell, his face turned towards the morning sun, a symbol of his unwavering spirit and dedication to his ideals.

At 7:03 a.m., the life of Jose Rizal, patriot, scholar, and inspiration to many, came to an end.
